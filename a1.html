Posted by Anze Wang on November 7, 2015

The Way to Become a OIer

The competitive programming experience has changed my life a lot since I was in senior high school. I joined the competitive programming club, Olympiad in Informatics (OI) club in the first week of school. It was a tough time for me to start gaining college level programming knowledge since I had just graduated from junior high school and I had some unpleasant experiences. To be honest, it is the happiest time in my life. I found the way to enjoy that hard time with my new friends. We discussed the new algorithms, and practiced relevant problems on various online judge systems. When we were working on problems, we could not even notice the time passing by.

Even though I made several close friends during that time, It dawned on that I let several opportunities of enjoying my senior high school time with my classmates slip from my hands. Since I spent a disproportionate amount of time in the computer lab, I forgone the opportunity to be with my classmates. When I stayed in classroom, I felt that I was excluded from the rest of them. Some of my class often teased me, “why don’t you go to the computer lab?” The question, “How could I be if I did not choose to walk on a differ path with my classmates”, always haunted me during high school. Although this question lingered in my mind, I never regretted my choice. Truly, that sound bothered me, but it never brought any regret to me. By learning competitive programming, I learned to study by myself, and learned to handle pressures in life.

Learning new knowledge is one of the most interesting experience during high school life. My self-studying skill was largely improved by learning a huge number of materials in a short interval. During the winter training camp of our club, we were assigned several topics about some algorithms or data structures. We should learn these advanced topics on our own and give a lecture to our classmates. The coach said that this is one of the most efficient way for us to study in a group. My topic was a data structure called Fenwick tree. This is a data structure which maintains a range sum of a set of data. It supports dynamic insertion and deletion of data in the set. The Fenwick tree is a data structure based on divide and conquer and complex bit operation. Although this is not a hard topic for most experienced programmer, it was extremely difficult for me as a beginner. At that time, I only had few experiences about “divide and conquer” and binary trees. I spent few hours reading research articles and sample code of Fenwick trees. To understand how does this structure work, I drew tens of graphs to show the data flow step by step. After struggling for a long time, I finally fully understood this data structure and gave a well-prepared lecture about Fenwick tree. To be honest, at the beginning, I could not understand why our coach ask us to learn these topics by ourselves. I considered learning an unfamiliar topic wastes a lot of time. To prepare for the competition, we should learn the algorithm from teacher in short time and use more time to practice hard problems. However, when the camp ended, I got interest in studying by myself. I began to enjoy learning hard problems and sharing them with my friends.

As claimed above, unpleasant experiences did exist. I learned to deal with the pressure which normal students may never need to face. In China, the Olympiad in Informatics is not considered as “useful” as other subjects, such as math, physics, and biology. These subjects can help students get a higher score in the Gaokao directly, the National Higher Education Entrance Examination, since they are Gaokao’s subject, but programming is not. My teacher tried to persuade me to give up OI several times, because, in his opinion, getting higher score in Gaokao is much more important than “giving efforts to gain useless knowledge.” Although I understood that the teacher wanted the best for me in his own way, I did not want to give up to do what I love. In addition, I felt students who are participating in competitive programming were not given the same privilege as students who are competing in math or physics Olympiads. The teacher allowed math or physics students to practice during class time, but chose to confiscate my algorithm book when I was practicing as well. I spent majority of time attempting to persuade my teacher, but to him, grades were the only thing that mattered. There was a period when I stayed in the computer lab all day long and never paid attention in class. My rank in the class dropped quickly. Even though I had a relative strong grasp of the fundamentals, my exam scores were horrible. Due to this incident, my teacher prohibited me from going to computer lab. To use the computer lab again, I tried to balance school work and learning algorithms. Spending twelve hours in school and practicing algorithms after school was exhausting, but I never considering giving up programming. Fortunately, I ranked in the top 10 of my class during the next exam. Soon, I earned the right to use computer lab again. Learning OI is a very hard path, we should not only endure the misunderstanding of teachers and classmates, we should also face to risk of fail in the competition. Although some students are admitted by prestigious universities due to their impressive competition results, most of us needed to go back to school and face Gaokao. If one does not have the ability to handle this pressure, he or she may quit soon.

When looking back on my competition life, I often wonder what would happen if I gave it up. I donated most of time to learning these complex and “useless” knowledge. However, I do not think that competitive programming is waste of time. When I started college life, I began at a higher level than my classmate. I have learned most of the computer science lower division knowledge. When others struggling with the difficult concepts, I could solve them easily and used my time to do more challenging tasks. Finally, all of the unpleasant experiences and pressures become good memories.
